Intel Sustainability Timeline

Explore the key milestones in Intel’s journey toward a more sustainable future. Discover how our commitment to innovation has shaped a more sustainable future for technology and our planet!

Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore establish Intel in Silicon Valley, setting the stage for decades of innovation in microprocessors and technology.

1968

Intel is Founded

Intel introduces the world’s first commercial microprocessor, the 4004, revolutionizing computing and enabling the development of personal computers.

1971

First Microprocessor (4004) Launches

Intel becomes one of the first tech companies to start voluntarily reporting its environmental impact, laying the groundwork for future sustainability initiatives.

1994

Begins Voluntary Environmental Reporting

Intel becomes the largest voluntary corporate purchaser of green power in the U.S. under the EPA’s Green Power Partner Program, significantly reducing its carbon footprint.

2008

Largest Corporate Purchaser of Green Power

Intel opens its first LEED-certified building, a design center in Haifa, Israel, demonstrating its commitment to sustainable architecture and energy efficiency.

2010

First LEED-Certified Building

Intel reduces its absolute greenhouse gas emissions by more than 60% below 2007 levels, surpassing its 20% reduction goal ahead of schedule.

2012

Major Greenhouse Gas Reduction

Intel signs the U.S. White House Climate Pledge, committing to ongoing green power purchases and increased use of on-site alternative energy sources.

2015

White House Climate Pledge

By 2023, Intel achieves 99% renewable electricity globally, a 43% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from its 2019 baseline, and net positive water use in four countries, including the U.S. and India.

2023

99% Renewable Electricity and Net Positive Water

Intel sets ambitious goals for 2030 and beyond: achieving 100% renewable electricity, net-zero greenhouse gas emissions in operations by 2040, reducing product carbon footprints, and increasing product energy efficiency tenfold.

2030 & Beyond

Net-Zero and Sustainable Product Goals
